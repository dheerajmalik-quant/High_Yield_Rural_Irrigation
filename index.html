The Old Way: The 35ft Cavity Well

Visualizing the "suction" limitations of the current centrifugal setup

Current State: 35ft Cavity Well.

Centrifugal pumps rely on atmospheric pressure. At 35ft, the physics of suction begin to fail.

As the localized water dome drops during pumping, cavitation occurs, efficiency plummets, and the pump struggles to pull water, throttling our yield.

5

The New Way: The Strainer Well

A cross-section of the 80-150ft deep rotary-drilled plan

Future State: Rotary-Drilled Strainer Well (80-150ft).

By bypassing the shallow clay and dropping a slotted PVC strainer pipe deep into the primary aquifer, water enters passively from all sides.

A submersible pump pushes water up, rather than sucking it, completely eliminating atmospheric lift limitations.

Proving 10" Hole - 6" Pipe = 2" Annular space

Geometry dictates flow efficiency.

  • Borehole Diameter: 10 inches
  • Casing Pipe Diameter: 6 inches

This leaves a mathematically perfect 2-inch annular gap wrapping the entire circumference of the pipe.

This gap is our engineering control zone, critical for the gravel pack.

The Porosity Catch: How Bajri Works

Visualizing why "rocks" don't block water (The Sponge Effect)

We fill the 2-inch annular gap with pea-gravel (Bajri).

Counter-intuitively, packing rocks around the pipe increases flow. The Bajri acts as a highly porous 3D sponge.

It filters out fine sand that destroys pumps, while the spaces between the round gravel allow pure water to flow rapidly into the strainer without friction.

Future-Proofing: Compressor Cleaning

Cleaning the well without ever pulling the pipe

Asset maintenance is built into the design.

Over years, silt can build up. Instead of pulling heavy iron pipes, we utilize high-pressure air compressors.

Air is forced down the well, violently agitating the water, flushing out sediment through the top without heavy mechanical lifting. The asset is permanently serviceable.
